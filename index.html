The Truth About Kitchen

How Banana Yoshimoto uses something as simple as a household plant for a symbol for tranquillity in her famous novel.

Rienan Mynn
rienanmynn🙄✌

14 min read

Coauthored by Carl Ton

Plants on a windowsill.

Yes — the ending to Kitchen by Banana Yoshimoto is horrendous. In all honesty, it was abrupt and overall just unsatisfying. But that's not what I'm here to discuss today. In fact, Kitchen is still one of my favorite books, and I will tell you all about why in just a second.

Hi, I'm Rienan Mynn and I'm a book enthusiast. I'm a student at Harvard Law and reading is one of my favorite things to do. I believe that, although Kitchen's plot isn't the greatest, the beautiful way emotion is expressed in such a simple text makes up for it. The book's poignant exploration of grief and loneliness is so powerful.

One of the best examples to demonstrate this is how Yoshimoto transforms a simple household item — plants — into a powerful metaphor for tranquillity. That's the focus of this article... just plants. But trust me, the sheer brilliance of Yoshimoto's writing here will not disappoint you.

Theme Statement

Plants are used repeatedly throughout the book, eliciting a sense of tranquility and emphasizing the importance of finding joy in small things throughout Mikage's turbulent life.

I've found eight quotes in Kitchen that demonstrate this the best. Each is in a different section of the book, just to vary things up a bit. Let's get into it.

1 — More Than Just Living

Section 1 · Pages 3–16

"Suspended in the dim light before the window overlooking the magnificent tenth-floor view, the plants breathed softly, resting."

— p. 16

Yoshimoto uses personification, describing the plants breathing, overlooking the window, and resting. Specifically, the plants even seem to share many of Mikage's emotions. In this part of the book, Mikage is exhausted and going to bed, so she imagines the plants resting and breathing softly as well. The plants represent companions for Mikage, making her feel less alone and helping her overcome the immense grief from losing her grandma. Moving into Yuichi's apartment finally makes Mikage feel like she belongs: she sleeps next to the kitchen (her favorite place), is next to plants that 'understand' her emotions, and is beside the room of Yuichi (who also had a close relationship with Mikage's grandmother).

Houseplants.

2 — The Warmth of Sunlight

Section 2 · Pages 17–30

"The plants near the window, enveloped in the gentle sunlight, sparkled bright green; far off in the pale sky, thin clouds gently flowed, suspended. It was a warm, lazy afternoon."

— p. 18

Yoshimoto uses visual imagery of plants to create a peaceful mood. After moving in with Yuichi and Eriko, Mikage finally feels a sense of comfort and her loneliness begins to decrease. The warm and friendly environment contrasts the scene before Mikage met Yuichi, where she was cold and shivering next to the refrigerator. The emphasis of sunlight on the plants is also a form of symbolism. Sunlight is a type of nutrient for the plant and essential for its wellbeing, paralleling how the Tanabe family's support is essential for Mikage's wellbeing.

Plants in warm light.

3 — Eriko's Graceful Hands

Section 3 · Pages 30–43

"Still, enveloped in the twilight coming from the west, there she was, watering the plants with her slender, graceful hands, in the midst of a light so sweet it seemed to form a rainbow in the transparent water she poured."

— p. 42

Yoshimoto uses imagery of twilight and plants to characterize Eriko as an elegant and graceful character. The description of the light as "so sweet" is figurative language, as the positive connotation of sweetness represents how Mikage feels warmth and comfort near Eriko. The rainbow that seems to form is also symbolism since its vibrant colors are often used to represent happiness and beauty, both of which Mikage associates with Eriko. Additionally, the act of watering plants itself is a demonstration of Eriko being a caring person. The trait of Eriko being caring is highlighted throughout the book to highlight the theme of family: though Eriko is biologically male and raising Yuichi alone, thus being an unconventional parent, she manages to be loving and supportive.

Watering plants.

4 — Silhouettes and Memory

Section 4 · Pages 44–56

"Night was just as it had been—here I was in the same room, the silhouettes of the plants in front of the terrace window looking down over the city."

— p. 53

In this quote, Yoshimoto uses techniques such as detailed optical imagery and a reflective tone to emphasize the importance of the flowers. By detailing how the plants are part of her memory of Eriko's apartment, she correlates them with happy memories, which contrast with the overarching melancholy tone of the book. Furthermore, by emphasizing their surroundings, she directs the reader's attention to the plants as a part of Eriko's legacy, which contributes to the story's overall message about how people can affect others posthumously.

• • •

5 — Joy in a Tomato

Section 5 · Pages 57–68

"At the supermarket I would stare at a bright red tomato, loving it for dear life. Having known such joy, there was no going back."

— p. 59

Yoshimoto begins with detailed, vivid imagery to help the reader envision the tomato's radiance. Then, she appeals to the reader's sense of happiness by correlating it with her positive emotions in order to demonstrate how plants create sources of joy for Mikage. By emphasizing its bright red nature (a color which is traditionally linked to life and vitality) and Mikage's abundance of love for the plant, the tomato also serves as a symbol of hope and elation, in contrast to the gloomy mood that follows Eriko's death.

Tomatoes on a vine.

6 — A Pineapple's Loneliness

Section 6 · Pages 74–86

"The sharp leaves stuck my death. In this world, tonight, only the pineapple and I understand each other— that thought came straight from my heart. Closing my eyes, as if against the cold wind, I felt we were the only two living things sharing the loneliness."

— p. 81

Yoshimoto's use of personification within Mikage's internal monologue emphasizes Mikage's perceived sense of loneliness, in which she is so unable to find human connection, that she imagines the pineapple as sharing her mutual sense of loneliness. She also uses personification to characterize the pineapple as a sentient being capable of feeling loss, which demonstrates the healing nature of plants, a theme that is frequently seen throughout the book.

A pineapple.

7 — Cutout Landscapes

Section 7 · Pages 87–94

"We passed by the silhouettes of trees and fields and mountains, like cutout pictures."

— p. 94

In this quote, Mikage talks about her impulsive taxi journey to see Yuichi, who had left following Eriko’s death. Here, Yoshimoto’s use of simile underlines the surrealness of Mikage’s journey, in which she is unable to ground herself in the reality of her surroundings, revealing her deep feelings for Yuichi. The significance placed on plants serves as a recurring motif, linking to Eriko’s own attachment to plants, who despite her death, still acts as a driving force for Mikage’s search for connection.

Trees and fields.

8 — An Eerie Illumination

Section 8 · Pages 95+

"The whole waterfall was illuminated here and there by amazingly bright green lights that brought the trees into almost unnatural relief."

— p. 95

Yoshimoto uses imagery to cast the scene through a seemingly eerie yet magical perspective. In this moment, Mikage deviates from her traditional long-held relationship with plants. The almost contradictory description of the trees as being unnatural, creates a sense of tension within the text. This establishes Mikage as emotionally unresolved as she approaches her vital reunion with Yuichi. Additionally, the relief Mikage feels within the eerie setting is symbolic of her newfound contentment with the unknown. Despite feeling lost and alone following Eriko’s death, she is able to persevere and search for meaning within her life. This contrasts Mikage’s initial helplessness after her grandmother’s death in the beginning of the book, revealing a gained sense of maturity and growth as a character.
